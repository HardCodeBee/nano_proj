Overview
You will train a small language model for story generation using nanoGPT. The assignment has four parts: a warm-up (not graded), training on ROCStories, and explorations.
Additionally, there will be an optional a story-generation competition.
Warm-up: Setup and First Run (0 points)
This part is not graded. Its goal is to make sure you can set up the environment and
understand the nanoGPT code structure.
• Follow the official nanoGPT README to install dependencies and verify your environment.
• Run a provided/official training or sampling command from nanoGPT to confirm your
setup works end-to-end.
• Try the provided eval.py script to evaluate a checkpoint and get a basic sense of
model quality.
• Write down brief notes (for yourself) about: code structure, where the dataset is
prepared, where training happens, and how sampling is done.

Task 1: Train nanoGPT on ROCStories (7 points)
Train your own nanoGPT model on the ROCStories training set. You may use the
ROCStories test set for local evaluation and model selection. We will use our own
held-out data for final grading, so please avoid overfitting to the public test set.
Requirements
(i) Data processing: Convert ROCStories into a training corpus suitable for nanoGPT
(e.g., plain text) and document the preprocessing you used (tokenization, special tokens, concatenation strategy, story separators, etc.).
(ii) Training: Train a model from scratch. You must report your key hyperparameters
(model size, context length, batch size, learning rate schedule, number of steps/epochs,
seed, etc.).
(iii) Evaluation: Evaluate your model on the ROCStories test split using at least one
quantitative metric (e.g., loss/perplexity) and include qualitative samples.
Note that Task 1 is a basic one. It means that you do not have to make improvements
on data or architecture. We suggest to place your improvement in Task 2, where you can
continue working on story generation and/or try other tasks. But if you would like to make
fancy stuff in Task 1, it will also be welcomed (but no additional marks).
Task 2: Exploration (8 points)
This task is open-ended. The goal is to encourage exploration beyond the base setup. You
can try different datasets or model variants. Possible directions include:
• Dataset ideas: Q&A, dialogue, or structured text such as recipes and instructions.
• Model ideas: Alternative model architectures (e.g., LLaMA-style blocks).
• Example idea: Try a dataset of SVG icons or simple vector drawings and train the
model to generate SVG code (e.g., basic shapes, logos, or glyphs).
Please include what you tried, why you chose it, and a short summary of what worked or
did not work in the Task 1 report.
We will score this task based on the originality of the exploration, the clarity of your
write-up, and whether your findings are supported by evidence (e.g., metrics or sample
outputs).

Report
Your report should summarize Task 1 and Task 2. It should clearly describe:
• Your data pipeline and any cleaning decisions.
• Training setup and compute budget (hardware and approximate training time).
• Learning curves (training/validation loss) and what you learned from these curves.
• Test-set evaluation results (numerical) and a brief error analysis (common failure
modes).
• Generated samples under different decoding settings (e.g., temperature/top-k), with a
short discussion.
• You may choose to put only a few generated samples in the main report and leave the
other samples to the Appendix, which comes after references.
• The main report should be no longer than 2 pages, excluding references. That is, references can come after the 2 pages. There is no page limit for references and Appendix
that comes afterwards. Do not expect we read the Appendix carefully, so don’t put
important things there, perhaps only some generation samples.

Marking Criteria
Task 1 will be assessed holistically across the following components:
• (2 mark) Pipeline correctness (data + training): Whether the dataset is processed
correctly, the training setup is sound, and the system runs end-to-end as described.
• (1 marks) Experimental rigor: Clear hyperparameter reporting, sensible baselines/ablation
choices (if any).
• (2 marks) Test performance: Quantitative metrics and qualitative story samples.
• (2 marks) Writing clarity: A well-structured, concise, and readable report with
enough details to follow your methodology and results. You should use plain English
and plain grammar. Over-length reports will be penalized by 1-2 marks, depending on
the extent. 1-2 marks will be deducted if any sentence is GPT written/polished but
not declared.

Task 3: (15 marks) Submit your best checkpoint for story generation
We will use our private test prompts to evaluate your model. We will use a combination of
PPL and Qwen scores for evaluation. Submission method can be seen in the last section of
this document.
Marking criteria: Your mark will be based on 1) whether you achieved a basic PPL value
(to be published, but would be around 20) and 2) your ranking in the class.
Additional Task 4: Story Generation Competition (optional, 2 extra points)
We will hold lab competitions to compare models by story quality. Note that you can
continue improving your model until the day before your lab.
• By the day before your Week 6 lab, you can choose to submit your trained checkpoint(s)
and a short description of your best configuration (data and model).
• We will provide prompts; your model will generate stories based on these prompts for
a head-to-head comparison.
• We will evaluate all submissions with human judgment (e.g., student voting), focusing
on coherence, creativity, consistency, and grammar.
• The winner student will have 2 extra marks for this course (total mark capped at 100).
